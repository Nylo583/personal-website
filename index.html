Fannergy

Aug 2022   •  PYTHON HTML CSS

My first completed Hackathon was for the World World Youth Coders Hackathon. I remember one of my friends (let’s call him S) proposed, as a joke, to create an AI fan for the theme of “Beating the Heat”. We called up our third group member in Canada (Mr. C for no other reason) and production was underway.

We had an entire week (wrong, we wasted 2 days on the idea) to create an integrated smart fan, complete with a website interface. I would handle the AI model and the website, C would work on the web server, and S would tackle sensing and hardware.

It took me around a day to set up the Tensorflow model in Python and train it on systematically generated data. The end goal of the Fannergy system was to spin the fan at a pace which would attempt to cancel out changes in room temperature and humidity and keep the room at a pleasant 70 degrees Fahrenheit. If I had more time, I would’ve pulled data from an API to make a better dataset, but I went with the “random-number-and-calculate” dataset generation method. In the end, it worked out with a 0.05% margin of error after 500 or so epochs of training.



It was now day 4 and time to get started on the website. Our team ended up deciding to create a home page, ID login, and a control board page which would either set a manual fan speed or give way to the AI to make decisions for the user. It would also include JS graphs of historical temperature and humidity readings. This was my first expedition into making websites, so it took me the great deal of the rest of the last 4 days to come up with a somewhat-pleasing-to-the-eye interface. See the demonstration below, or take a look at the screenshots below.

In the end, this project netted us $600 and the title of World World Youth Coders Hackathon winners!

Setsail

Sep 2022   •  GODOT

Wouldn't you like to have all the time in the world? Meet Bartholomeow, who spends his time sunbathing and counting down the days until his wooden rafter reaches his destination.



I've always been one to count down the days of school, summer vacation, and other spans of time. Thus, for my one-week-before-school procrastination project, I decided to give Bartholomeow a proper opportunity to be seen in a progress-tracker app.

I opened up Godot and got to work. My first goal was to tackle the actual progress screen. Bartholomeow would remain in the middle, floating back and forth on his raft, as hes traversed a procedurally-generated world. To accomplish this, I first made a generator and recycler which would hover off-screen, top and bottom respectively. I decided to implement OpenSimplexNoise in order to generate the world. Then, it was just adjusting the ranges for the land generation, and clearing the middle for our beloved cat to traverse along the river.

Zoomed-out view of the generator and recycler:

After that, things were very straightforward. I implemented a percentage through the date range using unix timestamps, and added some more visual niceties to the program. Finally, to wrap up my project rush week, I added a menu and basic goal-saving for convenience.





With perfect timing, I finished the day before my junior year of school started. Now, I'm sitting here, writing a devlog about it in the last week of that very same school year. Here, have a screenshot of Bartholomeow's journey at 99% completion, as well as a few more demos.

Sudoku

For my Create Task portion of AP Computer Science Principles, I decided to challenge myself a bit and create Sudoku using C++ (something I resoundly hate). A lot of the first few weeks was setting up my window and creating the game board, as I researched Sudoku generation algorithms and implementation. After finishing the graphical task of creating a Sudoku-esque board on the terminal window, I decided to create my own rendition of a Sudoku board generator which uses backtracking to fill the board according to the game's rules. Then, I saved a copy of that board, and removed most of the tiles to form the initial game board the user would play on. Here's a graphical representation of the algorithm I implemented, as well as how it turned out in the terminal at first.





I then displayed that board to the window, and inserted simple WASD (+ other keys) controls in order to traverse and edit the game board. As shown in the demo, I additionally coded an automatic error checking system which would highlight tiles which broke Sudoku rules in red. As a finishing touch, I added the option to generate a new board at any time using the backtracking algorithm from earlier, after seeing that the program was unusable once the game completed.

LSTM Stock AI

May 2023   •  PYTHON

School was starting to slow down, so I challenged myself to a one-week personal challenge. I had always wanted to predict stock prices using AI, so I did some research on LSTM (long-short term memory) AI regression models for stock prices and began work. Training the model was the easy part of this project. I simply downloaded ticker data from the yfinance library, then ran it through a Tensorflow LSTM model to get some pretty accurate results.



To having it predict future data, however, was quite tricky. First, I changed the test region of the model to look into the future. Then, I needed to store the model's predictions for use in future predictions as it starts to "look back" on its old estimates to generate more forecasts. The only problem was that the model would begin to crash its predictions to zero for any stock once it was only predicting from its own prior forecasting.



Either I raise the look-back period to accurately see more days, and have it be exponentially slower, or keep the look-back and efficiency as it was. Tough choice, but nevertheless, the model works beautifully at generating trendlines that rely solely on historical data.



If you want to compile it, you can here, just make sure you do not make real financial decisions with this model. It can't see the state of the market or ticker-specific happenings, and is therefore not a good representation of the future stock outlook.
